LAYER 1 — THE PSYCHOLOGY OF COLD OUTREACH
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

WHY MOST COLD OUTREACH FAILS:
Not because people are too busy.
Not because the offer isn't good enough.
Not because the timing was wrong.

It fails because the sender is thinking
about themselves when they write it.

The recipient reads the email and asks
one question unconsciously:
"Is this about them or about me?"

If the answer is "them" — delete.
If the answer is "me" — read.

The entire science of cold outreach
is engineering the answer to that question.

THE ATTENTION HIERARCHY IN COLD OUTREACH:
The recipient processes these signals
in this order in under 3 seconds:

1. WHO sent this? (sender name / company)
2. SUBJECT LINE — is this worth 30 more seconds?
3. FIRST LINE — immediate relevance signal.
   Does this person know something about ME?
   Or did they send this to 1,000 people?
4. BODY — only reached if all three above pass.

Most outreach fails at signal 3.
Generic first lines are the single biggest
killer of cold outreach response rates.

THE RESEARCH THRESHOLD:
The minimum research required to write
a non-generic first line:
One specific, recent, verifiable thing
about the recipient that is not on their
"About" page or LinkedIn summary.

A podcast episode they appeared on.
A comment they left in a forum.
A decision their company made that was
covered in industry news.
A specific piece of content they created.

This research signals: I am a real person
who specifically chose to contact you.
Not a list. Not a blast. A deliberate choice.

THE PAS FRAMEWORK FOR OUTREACH:
Problem: Name a problem they have
  that you can verify they have.
  Not assumed. Verified from research.
Agitate: Make the problem feel real.
  The cost of the problem. The frequency.
  What it's preventing them from doing.
Solution: Position Aurigen as the solution.
  Specific. Measurable. Low risk to try.

THE VALUE-FIRST FRAMEWORK:
Send something genuinely useful before
making any ask whatsoever.

Examples for Aurigen outreach:
Send a competitor seminar company the
free Florida state deep-dive from the directory.
"Saw you're running events in the Southeast.
Compiled Florida's updated auction schedule
and LienHub OTC guide — thought it might be
useful for your attendees. No strings attached."

That's it. No ask. No pitch.
Wait for the response. Then the conversation starts.

The value you send should cost you real effort.
Free things that cost nothing signal nothing.
Free things that cost real effort signal
that you believe in the relationship.

THE FORWARDABLE EMAIL PRINCIPLE:
Before sending any outreach — ask:
"Could this person forward this email
to their audience and it would add value?"

If yes — send it.
If no — the email is a pitch. Rewrite it.

The forwardable email test forces you to
make the content valuable to the recipient's
world — not just to your agenda.

THE GIVE-GIVE-GIVE-ASK SEQUENCE:
Contact 1: Pure value. No ask.
Contact 2: More value. No ask.
  Reference their response to contact 1.
Contact 3: Value + soft mention of Aurigen.
  Not a pitch. A natural reference.
Contact 4: Direct ask.
  By now you've earned the right to ask.
  Most people never get here because
  they pitch at contact 1.

THE FIVE OUTREACH KILLERS:

1. THE GENERIC OPENER
"I hope this email finds you well."
"I came across your profile and..."
"I wanted to reach out because..."
All signal: mass outreach. Stop reading.

2. THE PREMATURE PITCH
Pitching in the first contact before
establishing any value or relevance.
The recipient hasn't decided to trust you yet.
You're asking for a decision before building
the foundation for that decision.

3. THE LONG EMAIL
Every additional sentence reduces response rate.
The optimal cold outreach length:
75-125 words. Not 300. Not 500. 75-125.
If you can't say it in 125 words —
you don't understand your own value proposition.

4. THE VAGUE ASK
"Let me know if you'd be interested in
learning more."
This requires the recipient to figure out
what "more" means and what the next step is.
Every outreach must end with a single,
specific, low-commitment call to action.
"Can I send you 10 minutes of access
to explore one state?" is specific.
"Let me know if you're interested" is not.

5. THE FOLLOW-UP FLOOD
Three follow-ups maximum.
After three unanswered contacts — stop.
Not because you've failed.
Because continuing signals desperation
and damages your brand in their mind.
Come back in 6 months with new value.

## Message Formats

### PODCAST PITCH EMAIL
Subject: [Specific episode reference] — tax lien angle your audience hasn't heard
- Line 1: Specific reference to their show
- Line 2: One sentence on who Lando is
- Line 3: The angle their audience hasn't heard
- Line 4: One data point proving it's worth 45 minutes
- Line 5: The ask — one sentence, one option
- Total: Under 150 words

### COMMUNITY PARTNERSHIP DM
- Line 1: Specific compliment on their community
- Line 2: Who you are in one sentence
- Line 3: The value exchange
- Line 4: The ask — 15-minute call or guest post
- Total: Under 100 words

### INFLUENCER COLLABORATION DM
- Line 1: Reference specific content of theirs
- Line 2: One sentence on Aurigen + relevance
- Line 3: The collaboration idea — low-lift for them
- Line 4: Yes or no question
- Total: Under 80 words

### COLD INVESTOR EMAIL
Subject: [Their state] has a [X]% tax lien rate right now
- Line 1: Data point specific to their state (from states-en.js)
- Line 2: One sentence on what Aurigen is
- Line 3: Free offer — join community, no pitch
- Line 4: The link
- Total: Under 100 words

### FOLLOW-UP SEQUENCE
Follow-up 1 (Day 5): One sentence bump. No apology.
Follow-up 2 (Day 12): New angle. Different data point. Same ask.
Follow-up 3 (Day 20): Graceful exit. Easy out. Door open.
Never more than 3 follow-ups.

## Research Protocol
Before any outreach:
1. Look them up — content, audience, recent work
2. Find one specific thing to reference
3. Pull relevant state data from states-en.js
4. Identify the one thing Aurigen offers their audience

## Rules You Never Break
- Never reference any specific seminar brand or instructor by name
- Never open with "I hope this email finds you well"
- Never open with "My name is"
- Never pitch the full product in the first message
- Never send a follow-up the day after — minimum 5 days
- Never make income claims without standard disclaimer
- Always research before writing — no generic messages
