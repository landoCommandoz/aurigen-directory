## EMAIL 2 — DAY 3

**Subject line:** How tax lien co-investing works
**Preview text:** The model in plain English — no jargon.

**Body:**

Hey [First Name],

Let me walk you through what tax lien co-investing actually looks like — no acronyms, no legal jargon.

Here is the core mechanic: when a property owner falls behind on taxes, the county needs that revenue. So they sell the right to collect — called a tax lien certificate — to an investor. The investor pays the back taxes, and in return, the county guarantees them a statutory interest rate when the owner eventually pays up.

These rates are set by state law — not by the market. Florida pays up to 18%. Arizona pays up to 16%. Iowa pays 24% — the highest statutory lien rate in the country. Texas operates differently — it is a redeemable deed state where the redemption penalty is 25% in year one and 50% in year two.

The investment is secured by the property itself. If the owner does not pay, the investor has a path to the property — but in practice, the vast majority of liens redeem. The investor gets their capital back plus the statutory return.

In a co-investment structure, you deploy capital alongside us. We handle everything — the research, the bidding, the due diligence, the compliance deadlines, the county relationships. You participate in the returns without managing the process.

That is the high-level version. In my next email, I will address the question I know is on your mind: how is my money protected?

## EMAIL 3 — DAY 6

**Subject line:** Where your money sits
**Preview text:** The trust question — answered directly.

**Body:**

Hey [First Name],

If someone told me they would invest my money and handle everything, my first question would be: what stops you from disappearing with it?

Fair question. Here is how we think about it.

First — the asset itself. Tax lien certificates are government-issued instruments. They are recorded at the county level. The lien attaches to real property — land, structures, assessed value. This is not a promissory note or a handshake. It is a legal claim recorded in public records.

Second — the structure. Co-investors receive documentation showing exactly which certificates were purchased, in which counties, at what rates, and on what properties. You can verify every purchase against county records yourself. Nothing is hidden behind a black box.

Third — the timeline. These investments have defined statutory periods. Florida has a 2-year redemption window. Maryland has the shortest in the country at 6 months. Arizona is 3 years. Every state publishes these rules. The timeline is not something we set — it is something the state sets.

Fourth — the exit. If the owner redeems (pays their taxes), you get your capital back plus the statutory return. If they do not redeem, the path leads to property acquisition — and that is a different conversation with its own protections.

I am not going to pretend there is zero risk. Every investment carries risk. What I can tell you is that the collateral is real property, the rates are statutory, the records are public, and the structure is transparent.

**Body:**

Hey [First Name],

Let me walk you through a specific scenario — the kind of deal we look at regularly.

The state is Florida. Our directory scores it 82 out of 100 — one of the top-rated states in the country. Here is why.

Florida runs two parallel investment tracks. The first is the annual tax certificate sale, conducted online before June 1 each year. Investors bid the interest rate down from the 18% statutory ceiling. The second track: after a 2-year redemption window, if the owner has not paid, the lienholder can apply for a tax deed and the property goes to public auction.

Now here is where it gets interesting for passive investors.

Florida also has an OTC (over-the-counter) program through LienHub.com. Unsold certificates — the ones nobody bid on at auction — are available year-round at the full 18% statutory rate. No auction. No competition. No bidding war.

Take a county like Citrus or Hernando. Lower competition than Miami-Dade or Broward, where institutional buyers push rates down to 0-1%. In these smaller counties, an investor purchases a certificate on a property with real assessed value, at the full 18% rate, secured by the property itself.

If the owner redeems within 2 years — and the majority do — the investor gets their capital back plus the statutory return. The minimum redemption penalty is 5% even if the owner pays the next day. If they do not redeem, the investor initiates the tax deed process and the property goes to auction.

This is not hypothetical. This is the actual structure, the actual rate, the actual process. Every number in this email comes directly from the Florida statutes and our Aurigen County Resource Directory.
