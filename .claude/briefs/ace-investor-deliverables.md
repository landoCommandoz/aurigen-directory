## 1. CLOSING FRAMEWORK — The Passive Investor

For the person who says: "I don't want to learn all this. I just want to invest my money with someone who already knows what they're doing."

---

**THE SITUATION:**

Someone just sat through a presentation on tax liens and tax deeds. They're impressed by the data — the rates, the statutory backing, the county-level mechanics. But their eyes glazed over when you got into the how-to. They're not here to become a tax lien investor. They're here because they have capital sitting somewhere earning nothing, and they want someone competent to put it to work.

They may approach you after the talk or raise their hand during Q&A with something like, "This sounds great, but I don't have time to do all of this myself — can I just invest with you?"

That is the opening. Don't fumble it.

---

**THE APPROACH:**

Walk up like a peer, not a salesperson. They already told you what they want — respect that.

"Hey — I caught what you said a minute ago, and honestly, I appreciate the directness. Most people in this room want to learn how to do this themselves, and that's great. But you're telling me something different. You've got capital, you want it working harder, and you'd rather partner with someone who's already in the trenches than spend the next six months learning county treasurer websites. Do I have that right?"

Let them talk. They'll tell you everything you need to know in the next 60 seconds.

---

**THE DISCOVERY:**

Three questions. Ask them in order. Do not pitch until you have answers to all three.

1. "What's your capital doing right now — is it in the market, sitting in savings, in a self-directed IRA, or somewhere else?"

   Why this matters: You need to know their current vehicle and their liquidity. If they say "savings account" or "money market," they're frustrated with yield. If they say "IRA," you need to know if it's self-directed. If it's in the market, they're looking for diversification or downside protection.

2. "When you say you want to invest passively — what does that actually look like for you? Are you looking for a fixed return on your money, or do you want to own the underlying asset?"

   Why this matters: This separates the person who wants a predictable yield from the person who wants property acquisition. Two different conversations.

3. "What's the timeline — are you looking to deploy this capital in the next 30 days, or is this more of a 'right opportunity, right time' situation?"

   Why this matters: If they say 30 days, they're ready. If they say "right time," they need nurturing. Don't pitch a 30-day close to a 90-day buyer.

---

**THE PITCH:**

Tailor this based on their answers. Here's the core frame — adjust the specifics.

"Here's what we do. We research tax lien and tax deed opportunities across the states with the strongest statutory protections — places like Florida, where you can earn up to 18% backed by real property with a 2-year redemption window. Or Texas, where the redemption penalty is 25% in year one and 50% in year two — and that's written into the state statute, not a promise from a fund manager. Iowa pays 24% annually on liens. Arizona caps at 16%.

These aren't theoretical returns. These are statutory rates set by state legislatures. The lien is secured by the property itself — if the owner doesn't pay, the property transfers.

What we bring to the table is the research, the county-level due diligence, and the execution. We know which counties to target, which platforms to use, which properties to avoid. You bring the capital. We do the work. And everything is transparent — you see the liens, you see the counties, you see the numbers.

This isn't a fund where your money disappears into a black box. You know exactly where every dollar goes because it's tied to a specific property in a specific county in a specific state."

---

**THE CLOSE:**

One ask. One action.

"Look — I'm not going to ask you to decide anything right now. What I am going to ask is this: let's get on a 20-minute call this week where I can walk you through the specific structure, answer your questions about how the capital is deployed, and we can both figure out if this is a fit. If it's not — no hard feelings, and you still walk away knowing more about tax liens than 99% of investors. Fair enough?"

Or text it to them on the spot.

---

**IF THEY STALL:**

"I hear you. And honestly, if you're not sure, the worst thing you could do is rush into it. Here's what I'd suggest — book the call anyway. Use it as a fact-finding conversation. You'll ask me hard questions, I'll give you straight answers, and at the end of 20 minutes you'll either feel good about moving forward or you'll know it's not for you. Either way, you win. But don't let this sit in the 'I should look into that someday' pile — because you and I both know how that goes."

---

**IF THEY SAY NO:**

"Respect that completely. Here's what I'll do — I'll send you a link to our free resource directory. It covers all 50 states, rates, redemption periods, platforms, county-level data. If you ever want to explore this on your own or circle back to the co-investment conversation, everything you need is in there. And my number's on the card — use it whenever."

Give them the directory link. Keep the door open. Half of passive investor closes happen 2-8 weeks after the first conversation.

## 2. OBJECTION HANDLERS — Passive Investor Set

---

### OBJECTION 1: "How do I know my money is safe with you?"

**WHAT THEY REALLY MEAN:**
"I've trusted people with money before. Some of them earned it. Some of them didn't. I need to know you're not the second kind."

**ACKNOWLEDGE:**
"That's the right question to ask. If you didn't ask it, I'd be worried about you as an investor. Anyone who hands over capital without asking how it's protected deserves what happens next — and you're clearly not that person."

**REFRAME:**
"Here's what makes this different from most investment conversations you've had. In a tax lien, your money isn't protected by my word or my track record — it's protected by state statute and real property. When you buy a tax lien in Florida, the state of Florida says the property owner owes you up to 18% interest, and if they don't pay, you have a path to the property itself. That's not my promise. That's Florida Statute 197.

In Iowa, the statutory rate is 24% annually. In Arizona, it's up to 16%. In Texas, the redemption penalty is 25% in year one. These rates aren't set by me — they're set by state legislatures and backed by the property as collateral.

So the question isn't really whether your money is safe with me. The question is whether your money is safe in a structure where the collateral is real estate and the return is written into law. And that answer is in the statute."

**PROOF:**
- Florida: Up to 18%, 2-year redemption, score 82/100, beginner-friendly. Statute: FL 197.
- Iowa: 24% statutory rate, 2-year redemption, score 74/100. Statute: IA Code §446.
- Texas: 25% year-one / 50% year-two penalty on redemption, score 81/100. Statute: TX Tax Code §34.21.
- Arizona: Up to 16%, OTC liens available year-round, score 78/100. Statute: ARS §42-18053.

**REDIRECT:**
"Let me ask you this — what's the current yield on wherever your capital is sitting right now? And what's securing it?"

---

### OBJECTION 2: "What kind of returns are we talking about?"

**WHAT THEY REALLY MEAN:**
"Give me a number. But also — if the number sounds too good, I won't believe you. And if it sounds too low, I'm not interested."

**ACKNOWLEDGE:**
"Fair question. And I'm going to give you a straight answer instead of a range so wide it's meaningless."

**REFRAME:**
"Returns in tax liens are statutory — meaning the state sets the rate, not the market and not me. So when I tell you Iowa pays 24% annually on a tax lien certificate, that's not a projection or a best-case scenario. That's the Iowa Code. When I tell you Texas charges property owners a 25% penalty if they redeem in year one and 50% in year two, that's the Texas Tax Code.

Now — the realistic picture. In competitive counties, institutional buyers bid rates down. In Maricopa County, Arizona, liens that start at 16% get bid down to 0-3% because hedge funds are in the room. That's why we don't play in Maricopa. We target Pinal County, Yavapai County — places where the full 16% is achievable because the competition isn't there.

Same in Florida. Miami-Dade? Rates get bid to near zero. Citrus County, Hernando County? Much better. That's where the research matters — and that's what you're paying us to do.

So the honest answer: statutory rates range from 8% to 24% depending on the state, and your actual yield depends on the county-level strategy. That's why we have a directory covering all 51 jurisdictions — so we're always deploying into the best risk-adjusted opportunities."

**PROOF:**
- Iowa: 24% statutory rate (highest lien rate in the country), score 74
- Georgia: 20% year one + 10% per year after, score 71
- Florida: Up to 18% (5% minimum on redemption), score 82
- New Jersey: 18% + penalties, score 76
- Arizona: Up to 16%, OTC available year-round at full rate in rural counties, score 78
- Indiana: 10% (0-6 months) / 15% (6-12 months), score 69

**REDIRECT:**
"Here's the more important question — what return would you need to see to make this worth your time? Because if it's 6-8%, we can get you there with the lowest-risk lien states. If you're looking for 15%+, we target differently. What's the number that makes you lean forward?"

---

### OBJECTION 3: "What's the minimum investment?"

**WHAT THEY REALLY MEAN:**
"I might be interested, but I don't want to find out I need $500K to play. Or — I have a lot of capital and I want to know you can actually deploy it."

**ACKNOWLEDGE:**
"Good question. And the answer depends on which side of the spectrum you're on."

**REFRAME:**
"Tax liens are one of the few asset classes where you can start small and scale. Individual liens can be as low as a few hundred dollars — in Iowa, you can buy liens on rural parcels for under $500. In Arizona, OTC liens from county treasurers start at whatever the back taxes are owed — sometimes $200, sometimes $2,000.

For a co-investment relationship, we typically work with investors deploying $25,000 or more, because that lets us diversify across multiple liens in multiple states. If you're at $10,000, we can still have a conversation — but the strategy is narrower.

The real question isn't what the minimum is — it's what amount you're comfortable putting to work in a new asset class while you see how it performs. Most of the people I work with start with a portion of their available capital, see the first redemption come through, and then deploy more. That's smart investing. I'd rather you start with an amount that lets you sleep at night than an amount that has you checking your phone every morning."

**PROOF:**
- Arizona OTC: Liens available from county treasurers year-round, starting at the amount of back taxes owed (ARS §42-18122)
- Iowa: Annual auctions, individual liens can be under $1,000
- Indiana: Online auctions via SRI, liens start at back-tax amounts in Marion, Hamilton, Allen counties
- Florida: Liens available through county tax collectors, wide range of investment sizes

**REDIRECT:**
"So let me ask — are we talking about a first test with a smaller amount, or do you have a larger allocation you're looking to deploy? That changes the strategy conversation."

---

### OBJECTION 4: "How is this different from a fund or a REIT?"

**WHAT THEY REALLY MEAN:**
"I already have access to passive real estate investments. Convince me this is worth the mental energy of learning something new."

**ACKNOWLEDGE:**
"Honest question. And if you're already in REITs or syndications, you already know how passive investing works — so you're the right person to evaluate this."

**REFRAME:**
"Three differences that matter.

First — transparency. In a REIT, you own shares in a pool. You don't know which properties your money is in, you can't evaluate the underlying assets, and you find out how things are going when the quarterly report drops. In a tax lien co-investment, you know the exact county, the exact parcel, the exact lien amount, and the exact statutory rate. There's no abstraction layer.

Second — the return mechanism. A REIT's return comes from rental income and property appreciation — both market-dependent. A tax lien's return comes from a state-mandated interest rate or penalty that the property owner pays to redeem. It doesn't care what the stock market is doing. It doesn't care about occupancy rates. Iowa's 24% rate doesn't change because the Fed raised rates. Florida's 18% cap doesn't shrink in a recession. The return is statutory.

Third — collateral. In a REIT, if the fund underperforms, your shares lose value and you have no recourse against any specific asset. In a tax lien, if the owner doesn't redeem, you have a path to the property itself. The collateral is real, it's specific, and it's yours.

So the short version: a REIT is a stock that happens to hold real estate. A tax lien is a debt instrument secured by a specific property with a return set by law."

**PROOF:**
- Florida: Score 82, up to 18%, 2-year redemption, property-backed
- Texas: Score 81, 25%/50% penalty, hybrid system — path to deed if unredeemed
- Maryland: Score 77, 6-18% by county, 6-month redemption in Baltimore City at 18%
- Georgia: Score 71, 20% year one, 1-year redemption, hybrid with deed path

**REDIRECT:**
"What's your current REIT or fund returning right now — and do you know which specific properties your capital is in?"

---

### OBJECTION 5: "Can I pull my money out whenever I want?"

**WHAT THEY REALLY MEAN:**
"I'm afraid of getting locked in. If something goes wrong — in my life or with this investment — I need to know I can get out."

**ACKNOWLEDGE:**
"That's a smart thing to think about upfront. Anyone who tells you liquidity doesn't matter hasn't needed it in a hurry."

**REFRAME:**
"I'll be straight with you — this is not a savings account. When your capital is deployed into a tax lien, it's committed for the redemption period. That period varies by state. In Indiana, it's 1 year. In Florida, up to 2 years. In Iowa, 2 years. In Arizona, up to 3 years.

Now here's the upside of that: the reason the returns are what they are is precisely because the capital is committed. Iowa doesn't pay 24% because it's liquid — it pays 24% because you're locking in for 2 years. That's the trade.

What we do to manage this is diversify across states and redemption timelines. So you might have liens redeeming in 6 months in Maryland, 1 year in Indiana, and 2 years in Iowa. That creates a rolling return cycle where capital is always coming back — not all at once, but consistently.

But if you need the ability to pull everything out on 30 days' notice, this isn't the right vehicle. I'd rather tell you that now than have you find out later. The people who do well in this space are deploying capital they won't need for 12-24 months."

**PROOF:**
- Maryland: 6-month redemption (9 months owner-occupied), 6-18% rate
- Indiana: 1-year redemption, 10-15% rate
- Georgia: 1-year redemption, 20% year one
- Iowa: 2-year redemption, 24% rate
- Florida: 2-year redemption, up to 18%

**REDIRECT:**
"So let me ask you — of the capital you're considering, how much of it is money you truly won't need for 12 months or more? Because that's the piece we should be talking about. The rest stays liquid."

---

### OBJECTION 6: "I've been burned by partnerships before."

**WHAT THEY REALLY MEAN:**
"Someone I trusted took my money and either lost it, mismanaged it, or disappeared. I'm not doing that again."

**ACKNOWLEDGE:**
"I believe you. And I'm not going to pretend that doesn't change the way you evaluate every opportunity from here on out. It should. The fact that you're still in the room means you haven't given up on finding the right thing — you've just gotten more careful about who you trust. That's not a problem. That's wisdom."

**REFRAME:**
"Here's what I want you to notice about this conversation compared to whatever burned you before.

I'm not asking you to trust me. I'm asking you to trust a structure.

A tax lien is issued by a county government. The interest rate is set by the state legislature. The property is the collateral. If the owner redeems, you get your principal plus the statutory return — paid by the county, not by me. If they don't redeem, the property transfers through a legal process governed by state statute.

I don't hold your money in a checking account. I don't have discretion to 'invest' it wherever I feel like. Every dollar goes to a specific lien on a specific property in a specific county — and you can verify that through the county's public records.

The partnership that burned you — could you verify where your money was at any point? Could you look up a public record and confirm it? In this structure, you can. That's not a pitch. That's just how county government works."

**PROOF:**
- All liens are public record, searchable through county treasurer or assessor portals
- Florida liens: tracked through county tax collector, score 82
- Arizona liens: Maricopa treasurer portal (treasurer.maricopa.gov), full online transparency
- Indiana liens: SRI platform, Marion/Hamilton/Allen counties, all verifiable online
- Texas: County-level sheriff sales and deed records, all public

**REDIRECT:**
"If I could show you a structure where you can independently verify where every dollar of your capital is at any time — through a county government website, not through me — would that change the conversation?"

## 3. PHONE SCRIPT — Passive Investor Follow-Up Call

For someone who expressed interest in co-investing but didn't commit. Goal: qualify capital readiness and timeline without sounding like a financial interrogation.

---

### OPENING (5 seconds — get permission)

"Hey [Name], it's [Your Name] with Aurigen. You got a minute?"

If yes, continue. If no:

"No worries — when's a better time? I'll call you back then."

Set the callback and end the call. Do not pitch into a "not right now."

---

### BRIDGE (10 seconds — reference the event)

"I wanted to follow up from [the seminar / our conversation / the event]. You mentioned you were interested in the co-investment side of what we do with tax liens — putting your capital to work without having to become a full-time researcher. I wanted to pick that back up."

---

### REASON FOR CALL (10 seconds — specific, not generic)

"I'm not calling to sell you anything. I'm calling because we're putting together our next round of lien acquisitions and I wanted to see if the timing works for you — or if this is more of a down-the-road conversation."

---

### DISCOVERY (get them talking — 4 questions, asked naturally, not rapid-fire)

These are the four things you need to know. Weave them in. Do not ask them like a checklist.

**Question 1 — Status check:**
"Since we talked, have you had a chance to look into tax liens at all, or has it just been sitting on the back burner?"

This tells you their engagement level. If they researched, they're warm. If they forgot, they're cold and you need to re-establish value.

**Question 2 — Capital readiness:**
"You mentioned you had some capital you were looking to put to work. Is that still the case, or has anything changed on your end?"

Do not ask "how much." You're asking if the capital still exists and is still available. The amount comes later, in the clarity call.

**Question 3 — Timeline:**
"If everything checked out and you felt good about the structure — are we talking about something you'd want to move on in the next couple of weeks, or is this more of a Q2/Q3 conversation?"

This separates now-buyers from later-buyers. Both are valuable. But you sell them differently.

**Question 4 — Hesitation check:**
"Last time we talked, you seemed interested but you didn't pull the trigger. That's fine — but I'm curious, was there something specific that held you back? Something I didn't answer well enough, or something you wanted to think through?"

This is the most important question. Whatever they say next is the real objection. Use the objection handlers above.

---

### PIVOT TO VALUE (30 seconds — address what they said)

Based on their answers, deliver one of these:

**If they're warm and capital is ready:**
"Good — sounds like the timing might actually work. Here's what I'd suggest. Let's get you on a 20-minute Investor Clarity Call where I walk you through the specific states we're targeting right now — we've been focused on Florida, Iowa, and Arizona, where statutory rates run from 16% to 24% and the lien structure is the most investor-friendly in the country. I'll show you how the capital gets deployed county by county, what the expected redemption timeline looks like, and you can ask me anything. If it makes sense, we move forward. If not, you'll at least have the full picture."

**If they're warm but capital isn't ready yet:**
"That's fine — and honestly, timing matters more than urgency in this space. Liens aren't going anywhere. Here's what I'd suggest: let me send you our state directory so you can see the actual rates and structures across all 50 states. When the capital frees up, you'll already know how this works and we can move fast. Sound good?"

**If they're cold and need re-engagement:**
"No problem at all. Let me give you the quick version of why this came back on my radar. We just closed a round of lien acquisitions in Iowa at 24% and Indiana at 10-15%, both backed by real property. If you're still earning 4-5% on whatever your capital is in, it might be worth a 20-minute conversation just to see if this is a fit. If not — no pressure. But I didn't want to let it die without at least checking in."

## Coaching Notes

**Closing Framework:** The passive investor doesn't want to be educated — they want to be assured. Keep the mechanics brief and the structure transparent. If you spend more than 2 minutes explaining how tax liens work, you've lost them. They care about: where does my money go, what secures it, what's the return, and how do I verify it.

**Objection Handlers:** The #1 mistake with passive investor objections is getting defensive. These people have money. They've heard pitches. The moment you start justifying, you sound like every other person who lost their capital. Instead: acknowledge the concern, point to the structure (not yourself), and let the statute do the selling.

**Phone Script:** The call should feel like a check-in, not a close. If you're talking more than 40% of the time, you're doing it wrong. Ask the four discovery questions, listen to the answers, and match your value statement to what they told you. The booking link is the only close. Everything else is just conversation.
