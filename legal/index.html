CLASSIFICATION METHODOLOGY

The Aurigen County Resource Directory classifies each U.S. state and the District of Columbia into one of five categories based on the primary statutory mechanism available to investors at the point of entry:

  • Tax Lien — The investor purchases the tax debt (a lien certificate). The property owner retains ownership and has a statutory redemption period to repay the debt plus interest.
  • Tax Deed — The investor purchases the property itself at a public auction. Ownership transfers to the winning bidder upon completion of the sale.
  • Redeemable Deed — The investor purchases a deed, but the original owner retains a legal right to reclaim the property within a post-sale redemption window.
  • Hybrid — Counties within the state may conduct either lien sales or deed sales depending on local rules, statutes, or county-level elections.
  • Forfeiture — The state uses a forfeiture process in which property is surrendered to the state after multi-year delinquency and made available through a separate disposition process.

Our classifications are based on publicly available state statutes, county auction procedures, and official government resources. We review and update classifications periodically, but laws and procedures change. Always verify the current mechanism with the official county or state authority before transacting.

WHY DIFFERENT SOURCES LABEL STATES DIFFERENTLY

There is no single industry standard for classifying states. Different educators, platforms, and reference materials may label the same state differently depending on their methodology:

  • Some classify by investor entry point (what you buy at auction).
  • Some classify by investor exit point (what happens if the owner does not redeem).
  • Some use simplified two-category systems (lien vs. deed) that collapse redeemable deeds and hybrid states into one of those two buckets.
  • Some reflect county-level variations while others generalize at the state level.

None of these approaches are inherently wrong. The Aurigen Directory uses a five-category system that classifies by entry point because we believe this gives investors the clearest picture of what they are actually purchasing and what strategy applies.

If you see a discrepancy between our label and another source, both may be correct under different classification frameworks. We encourage you to read the underlying statute and confirm the process with the relevant county before making any investment decision.
